## I. Language and Tone

### Proposal Language Consistency
- [ ] All documents use proposal language ("would," "proposed," "upon approval")
- [ ] No operational language assuming active program ("is," "provides," "our students")
- [ ] Appropriate use of conditional phrasing ("could," "might," "would potentially")
- [ ] No definitive statements about uncommitted resources or partnerships

### Professional Quality
- [ ] Professional, academic tone throughout
- [ ] Free of grammatical and spelling errors
- [ ] Consistent terminology across documents
- [ ] Appropriate level of formality for audience

### Disclaimers and Context
- [ ] Appropriate disclaimers on operational/implementation sections
- [ ] Clear indication of proposal vs. operational planning
- [ ] Notes about finalization upon approval where appropriate
- [ ] Transparent about assumptions and contingencies

## Appendix: Common Issues to Avoid

### Language Pitfalls
❌ "The program provides..."  
✅ "The program would provide..."

❌ "Our students learn..."  
✅ "Students would learn..."

❌ "Canvas hosts our assessments..."  
✅ "Canvas could host assessments..."

❌ "Partners include Google and Amazon..."  
✅ "Potential partners could include Google and Amazon..."

### Operational Over-Specificity
❌ "Week 3: Students complete Module 5..."  
✅ "The proposed course structure would include progressive modules..."

❌ "Prof. Smith will teach A²I 601..."  
✅ "Core faculty would teach foundational courses..."

❌ "Budget: $1,247,390"  
✅ "Budget: $1.2M - $1.3M"

### Unsupported Claims
❌ "Students will earn $50K more within one year"  
✅ "Industry data suggests AI leaders earn $30-80K more than general managers; graduates would be positioned for these roles"

❌ "This is the best AI business program"  
✅ "This would be the first comprehensive business MS in AI orchestration"
